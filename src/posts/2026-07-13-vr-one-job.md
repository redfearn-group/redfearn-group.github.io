I've tried every serious headset since the early Rift kits, and I can tell you exactly where each generation improves: resolution, weight, field of view, passthrough quality. What hasn't meaningfully improved in a decade is the question that actually decides adoption: why put this on today, for this task, instead of just doing the task?

Hardware people keep solving hardware problems. The wall was never hardware.

## Comfortable isn't the same as invisible

A headset can be light enough to forget you're wearing it and still fail, because the friction isn't physical. It's contextual. Stop what you're doing, find the device, put it on, wait for it to boot, enter a separate mode of existence. Compare that to the phone already in your hand, or the monitor already in front of you. Zero setup cost.

Until entry cost drops close to zero, VR keeps winning the demo and losing the daily habit. Doubling the pixel density doesn't touch the fact that putting the thing on is a decision, not a reflex.

## Where it actually works today

The use cases with real, durable VR adoption share one property: the task needs spatial presence a flat screen genuinely can't provide. Industrial training on equipment too expensive or dangerous to practice on directly. Remote collaboration on physical, three-dimensional designs. Certain therapy and rehab, where the immersion is the mechanism, not the marketing.

General productivity, casual entertainment, social spaces competing with a phone: none of those made the list. They get reinvented every hardware cycle and keep failing to stick, because the friction problem never got addressed. Just decorated with better specs.

If you're building for VR right now, don't ask how to make it more immersive. Ask whether the task needs a headset at all, or whether you're solving a problem a phone already solves at zero setup cost. Most roadmaps haven't asked that honestly. It shows in the retention numbers.
