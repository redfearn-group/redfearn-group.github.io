I've tried every serious headset since the early Rift kits, and I can tell you exactly where each generation improves: resolution, weight, field of view, passthrough quality. What hasn't meaningfully improved in a decade is the answer to the question that actually decides adoption: why would I put this on today, for this task, instead of just doing the task.

Hardware people keep solving hardware problems. The wall was never hardware.

## Comfortable is not the same as invisible

A headset can be light enough to forget you're wearing it and still fail, because the friction isn't physical, it's contextual. You have to stop what you're doing, find the device, put it on, wait for it to boot, and enter a separate mode of existence to use it. Compare that to the thing it's replacing, which is usually a phone already in your hand or a monitor already in front of you requiring zero setup cost.

Until the entry cost drops to something close to zero, VR is going to keep winning the demo and losing the daily habit. That's not a resolution problem. Doubling the pixel density does not touch the fact that putting the thing on is a decision, not a reflex.

## Where it actually works today

The use cases where VR has real, durable adoption all share one property: the task itself requires spatial presence that a flat screen genuinely can't provide. Industrial training on equipment too expensive or dangerous to practice on directly. Remote collaboration on physical, three-dimensional designs. Certain categories of therapy and rehab where the immersion is the mechanism, not the marketing.

Notice what's missing from that list: general productivity, casual entertainment, social spaces competing with a phone. Those categories keep getting reinvented every hardware cycle and keep failing to stick, because the underlying friction problem was never addressed, just decorated with better specs.

If you're building for VR right now, the honest question isn't "how do we make this more immersive." It's "does this task actually need a headset, or are we solving a problem that a phone already solves at zero setup cost." Most roadmaps I see haven't asked that question honestly, and it shows in the retention numbers.
